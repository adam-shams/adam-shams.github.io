About Me

Who I am, where I come from, and what drives me.

I'm Adam Shams, a Computer Engineering student at the Georgia Institute of Technology. I grew up in Los Altos, California — in the heart of the Bay Area — surrounded by the energy of Silicon Valley. That environment shaped my fascination with technology early on: I've been building things since middle school, from robotics workshops at the Mountain View Library to full-scale AI systems presented at Apple.

What drives me is a passion for solving problems that have real human impact. I'm not interested in technology for its own sake — I want to build systems that help people. That's why I gravitate toward AI and machine learning: these tools have the power to transform healthcare, education, and how we make decisions. My research at the Bio-MIBLab, where I built clinical Q&A pipelines for ICU data, reinforced that conviction. So did my work on medical chatbots for AFib patients and diabetes risk education, which were presented at ENDO 2025.

At Georgia Tech, I'm in the Honors Program studying Computer Engineering with coursework in Data Structures & Algorithms and Digital System Design. Outside the classroom, I'm a member of AISI (the AI Safety Initiative), because I believe building powerful AI responsibly is just as important as building it at all. I also contribute to open-source projects — my multi-agent debate platform is now part of the official Langroid framework.

Before GT, I attended Khan Lab School in Mountain View, where I earned STEM Mastery with Distinction and taught Linear Algebra as a student instructor. I also completed 52 units of dual-enrollment college coursework. These experiences taught me how to learn deeply and communicate complex ideas clearly — skills I carry into every project I take on.

When I'm not coding, you'll find me playing football or basketball, working through a chess puzzle, building with Legos, or gaming. I believe the best engineers are well-rounded people, and these hobbies keep me sharp and creative.
